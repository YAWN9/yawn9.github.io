To photograph means “to draw with light.”

It all begins with a feeling, an impression, an idea. I photograph, therefore I capture. Time-consuming and difficult preparatory work is often necessary before the moment can be expressed in the way it was experienced.

Having succeeded in taking a “good” picture, the birth-giving process begins with a long sequence of darkroom sessions. Days, weeks, even months, can go by before the inner essence harmonizes with the external image. The aim is to develop and work on a fine print in the darkroom until perfection is achieved. Repeated attempts are necessary before the desired expression is obtained. Dodging, burning in and other techniques follow each other in an intuitive, coherent rhythm.

But often a fresh and rested eye will be a merciless critic the next day, because it cannot be deceived. It discerns imperfections and detects disharmony. Such high-quality standards result in only a few pictures ever reaching the intended level of fastidiousness.

Each of my fine prints is an original and handmade on the finest doubleweight baryta paper. All prints are specially treated with sulfuric or selenium toners to withstand environmental influences, ensuring maximum durability. The fine prints are then finished in my studio: they are dry-mounted on white, non-acid museum quality cardboard, fitted with a passepartout, retouched, and framed.

My preferred choice of camera is an 8×10 inch large format camera, the “queen” of all formats. Working with this size of camera requires careful reflection and leads to meditative work. 35 mm and medium-format cameras also have a place and are featured in my work.
